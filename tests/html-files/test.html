Course Description

PHY 2054 is the second of a two-semester sequence in introductory physics offered primarily for students majoring in information technology, the biological sciences and pre-health professions. Emphasis is placed on understanding major principles, and mathematics is used to clarify concepts. Students must have a good working knowledge of algebra and trigonometry. The course is quite intense and will require you to invest considerable time in study and problem solving.

Course Materials and Resources

Required Text

Online free excellent textbook: OpenStax College Physics is a free online beautiful textbook with lots of nice historical anecdotes and pictures. It also provides a variety of examples to make connection of the contents to real life applications which includes a large number of examples and problems from human health and medical physics.  You can download an app in your phone/tablet or download the text. The book is available on webcourses. I have prepared slides based on the materials from the chapters. I will upload the slides prior to the lectures to guide you to follow the book.

Student Learning Outcomes

By completing this course (along with Physics) you will master problem solving skills and will be able to connect the concepts to your future profession. A few  important learning outcomes are:

  • Appreciate that a charge like mass is a fundamental quantity and occurs in quanta, and that there are two kind of charges. There is a force of attraction or repulsion between any two charges  which can produce acceleration. By applying kinematics you will be able to figure out motion of charges particles.  
  • You will appreciate how a battery works and will be able to understand how your electric bill is calculated
  • You will appreciate how a radio or a TV works and when you switch from one station/channel what actually happens.
  • You will appreciate the properties of electromagnetic waves - that light wave, microwave, X-ray are all electromagnetic waves of different frequencies and wavelength, some of them can be potentially harmful when exposed to. You will appreciate the origin of colors in butterflies and paintings.

Course Activities

The course will be taught Face-to-Face in the lecture room MSB 0260, MTuWTh 2:00 PM - 3:50 PM for 6 weeks starting from May 15, 2022 - till June 22, 2022. The slides, handouts and homework problems will be posted on a weekly basis. To remain up-to-date and to do well you need to visit the course Webpage at least once every day.  Attending lectures, joining in discussions,  watching step-by-step problem solving during lecture will help you to master the concepts and do well in quizzes, tests, and the final.

Quizzes, Homework, & Tests

All reading quizzes (2 from each chapter) will be available online. Reading quizzes will have 10% weight on total score. All reading quizzes are open book and do  not require online proctoring. They are easy with two attempts. Highest score from two attempts will be final score for each reading quiz.

Online quizzes:  There will be open book online quizzes and practice quizzes. Taking these quizzes regularly will keep you at pace with the course.  Quizzes online and quizzes offer ed during lecture together will have 10% weight on total score. Online quizzes are open book, each will have two attempts, but will require lockdown browser with a link for the textbook. The average of two attempts will be the final score for each quiz.

 

Three tests (each 25%), (the 3rd test will be counted as the final) will take place during lecture period at MSB 0260. There is no other choice unless prior arrangement has been arranged and approved by the appropriate authorities, such as, SAS. 

The quizzes offered during lecture and the three tests will have a mix of free response and multiple choice questions. In addition you will have discussion quizzes (5% of the total grade) in your respective discussion sections.

The final (test 3) will take place at MSB 0260 during the last lecture ( Thursday June 22, 2:00 PM - 3:50 PM)

  • Homework: I will assign homework from each chapter for you to work on - but these will not be graded. I will post solutions to all the homework problems.  Nonetheless,  trying the homework on your own will be a key component to do well in the course, as quizzes, and the questions for the tests, and the final  will be very similar to these questions. I will work out some additional problems during lecture. You can take advantage of the discussion sessions to work and finish those problems in small groups with the help of the GTA.
  • Tests: There will three tests - each will carry 25% weight towards the final grade - a total of 75% of your final grade. The 3rd test will be considered as the final for this course. You need to be physically present at MSB0260 to take the tests unless you have made prior arrangement to take the test at the student testing center.
  • Discussion: The discussion section quiz offered in each section by the GTA will consists of 5% of the grade. You should make every effort to attend the discussion sections where you'll break up into small groups in problem solving sessions.  A graduate teaching assistant (GTA) will be in charge of each discussion section.  

Resources or actions

  • You should  plan on logging into the course everyday each week to look at the lecture notes posted after each lecture and/or complete the reading quizzes. If you do not possess a computer or a Notepad/IPad - you can borrow a laptop from the library and renew it. In the worst case, you can come to my office to take a quiz.
  • You  should plan on a couple of hours everyday to read the textbook, review the slides, and work through the worked out problems independently before trying the assigned homework problems.  
  •  I strongly encourage that you invest  time to work out some problems of your own and discuss among your friends, ask the discussion GTA during the discussion session.

Attendance/Participation

The course is offered face-to-face.  A minimum of 70% attendance is necessary to pass the course.

Make-up Exams and Assignments

Per university policy, you are allowed to submit make-up work (or an equivalent, alternate assignment) for authorized university-sponsored activities, religious observances, or legal obligations (such as jury duty). If this participation conflicts with your course assignments, I will offer a reasonable opportunity for you to complete missed assignments and/or exams. The make-up assignment and grading scale will be equivalent to the missed assignment and its grading scale. In the case of an authorized university activity, it is your responsibility to show me a signed copy of the Program Verification Form for which you will be absent, prior to the class in which the absence occurs. In any of these cases, please contact me ahead of time to notify me of upcoming needs. At the discretion of the instructor, the make-up may take any reasonable and appropriate form including, but not limited to the following: allowing a replacement exam, replacing the missed work with the same score as a later exam. All assignment and exam grades are final 72 hours after they have been returned. Please contact me before this 72-hour period is over if you have a grading dispute.

Policy Statements

Academic Integrity

Students should familiarize themselves with UCF’s Rules of Conduct. According to Section 1, "Academic Misconduct," students are prohibited from engaging in:

  • Unauthorized assistance: Using or attempting to use unauthorized materials, information or study aids in any academic exercise unless specifically authorized by the instructor of record. The unauthorized possession of examination or course-related material also constitutes cheating.
  • Communication to another through written, visual, electronic, or oral means: The presentation of material which has not been studied or learned, but rather was obtained through someone else’s efforts and used as part of an examination, course assignment, or project.
  • Commercial Use of Academic Material: Selling of course material to another person, student, and/or uploading course material to a third-party vendor without authorization or without the express written permission of the university and the instructor. Course materials include but are not limited to class notes, Instructor’s PowerPoints, course syllabi, tests, quizzes, labs, instruction sheets, homework, study guides, handouts, etc.
  • Falsifying or misrepresenting the student’s own academic work.
  • Plagiarism: Using or appropriating another’s work without any indication of the source, thereby attempting to convey the impression that such work is the student’s own.
  • Multiple Submissions: Submitting the same academic work for credit more than once without the express written permission of the instructor.
  • Helping another violate academic behavior standards.

For more information about Academic Integrity, students may consult The Center for Academic Integrity.

For more information about plagiarism and misuse of sources, see “Defining and Avoiding Plagiarism: The WPA Statement on Best Practices”.

Responses to Academic Dishonesty, Plagiarism, or Cheating

Students should also familiarize themselves with the procedures for academic misconduct in UCF’s student handbook, The Golden Rule. UCF faculty members have a responsibility for students’ education and the value of a UCF degree, and so seek to prevent unethical behavior and when necessary respond to academic misconduct. Penalties can include a failing grade in an assignment or in the course, suspension or expulsion from the university, and/or a "Z Designation" on a student’s official transcript indicating academic dishonesty, where the final grade for this course will be preceded by the letter Z. For more information about the Z Designation, see http://goldenrule.sdes.ucf.edu/zgrade.

Course Description

PHY 2054 is the second of a two-semester sequence in introductory physics offered primarily for students majoring in information technology, the biological sciences and pre-health professions. Emphasis is placed on understanding major principles, and mathematics is used to clarify concepts. Students must have a good working knowledge of algebra and trigonometry. The course is quite intense and will require you to invest considerable time in study and problem solving.

Policy Statements

Academic Integrity

Students should familiarize themselves with UCF’s Rules of Conduct. According to Section 1, "Academic Misconduct," students are prohibited from engaging in:

  • Unauthorized assistance: Using or attempting to use unauthorized materials, information or study aids in any academic exercise unless specifically authorized by the instructor of record. The unauthorized possession of examination or course-related material also constitutes cheating.
  • Communication to another through written, visual, electronic, or oral means: The presentation of material which has not been studied or learned, but rather was obtained through someone else’s efforts and used as part of an examination, course assignment, or project.
  • Commercial Use of Academic Material: Selling of course material to another person, student, and/or uploading course material to a third-party vendor without authorization or without the express written permission of the university and the instructor. Course materials include but are not limited to class notes, Instructor’s PowerPoints, course syllabi, tests, quizzes, labs, instruction sheets, homework, study guides, handouts, etc.
  • Falsifying or misrepresenting the student’s own academic work.
  • Plagiarism: Using or appropriating another’s work without any indication of the source, thereby attempting to convey the impression that such work is the student’s own.
  • Multiple Submissions: Submitting the same academic work for credit more than once without the express written permission of the instructor.
  • Helping another violate academic behavior standards.

For more information about Academic Integrity, students may consult The Center for Academic Integrity.

For more information about plagiarism and misuse of sources, see “Defining and Avoiding Plagiarism: The WPA Statement on Best Practices”.

Responses to Academic Dishonesty, Plagiarism, or Cheating

Students should also familiarize themselves with the procedures for academic misconduct in UCF’s student handbook, The Golden Rule. UCF faculty members have a responsibility for students’ education and the value of a UCF degree, and so seek to prevent unethical behavior and when necessary respond to academic misconduct. Penalties can include a failing grade in an assignment or in the course, suspension or expulsion from the university, and/or a "Z Designation" on a student’s official transcript indicating academic dishonesty, where the final grade for this course will be preceded by the letter Z. For more information about the Z Designation, see http://goldenrule.sdes.ucf.edu/zgrade.
